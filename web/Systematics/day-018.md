## Verse

"God is not man, that he should lie, or a son of man, that he should
change his mind. Has he said, and will he not do it? Or has he spoken,
and will he not fulfill it?" — Numbers 23:19

## Explanation

The doctrine of inerrancy isn't primarily about defending a book — it's
about defending God's own character. If Scripture is genuinely God's own
word, as we established weeks ago, then the question of whether it can
contain real errors isn't really a question about ancient manuscripts.
It's a question about whether God himself lies, misleads, or gets things
wrong. Numbers 23:19 answers that plainly: he does not, and he cannot,
because it would contradict who he is.

This is why the doctrine matters so much practically, not just
academically. If parts of Scripture could be false, then every reader
becomes, in practice, the final judge of which parts to trust and which
to set aside — and Scripture's authority, the very thing we spent last
week establishing, quietly collapses into whatever each reader already
prefers to believe. Inerrancy isn't a technical add-on to a high view of
Scripture; it's what makes a genuinely final, trustworthy authority
possible at all.

This should be a source of real stability, especially when you meet
Scripture at its hardest points — commands you don't understand, passages
that trouble you, apparent tensions between texts. The ground you're
standing on isn't your own ability to resolve every difficulty. It's the
character of the God who cannot lie.

## Prayer

Father, thank you that your trustworthiness, not my ability to explain
everything, is what makes your word reliable. Let that truth steady me
when I meet passages I don't yet understand. Amen.

## Reflection Question

When you encounter a difficult or confusing passage, does your confidence
rest more on your own ability to explain it, or on God's character behind
it?
