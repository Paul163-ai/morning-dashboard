## Verse

"In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word
was God." — John 1:1

## Explanation

John's opening line is deliberately, carefully constructed. "The Word was
with God" affirms real personal distinction — the Word (whom John
identifies as Jesus in v.14) is not simply identical to the Father, but
in relationship with him. "The Word was God" affirms, just as clearly,
full deity — not "a god," not "godlike," but God, in the fullest sense
the term carries throughout John's Gospel. Distinction and full deity,
stated together, in a single sentence.

This claim runs throughout the New Testament. Thomas calls the risen
Jesus "my Lord and my God" (John 20:28), without correction. Paul says
"in him the whole fullness of deity dwells bodily" (Colossians 2:9).
Hebrews calls the Son "the exact imprint" of God's nature, sustaining the
universe by his own powerful word (Hebrews 1:3). This wasn't a later
development smuggled into the faith centuries after Jesus — it's there,
explicit, from the earliest Christian writings, the necessary
consequence of who the first disciples came to recognise Jesus actually
was.

This matters immensely for the gospel itself. If Jesus were merely a
great teacher or exalted creature, his death could not accomplish what
Scripture says it accomplished. Only because the Son is fully God could
his sacrifice be of infinite worth, sufficient to atone for sin.

## Prayer

Lord Jesus, I confess you as fully God — not a lesser being, not merely a
great teacher, but God himself, who took on flesh for my salvation. I
worship you as Thomas did: my Lord and my God. Amen.

## Reflection Question

Does your everyday sense of who Jesus is match the full weight of "the
Word was God"? Where might it fall short?
