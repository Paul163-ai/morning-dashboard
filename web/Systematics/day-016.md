## Verse

"The heart is deceitful above all things, and desperately sick; who can
understand it?" — Jeremiah 17:9

## Explanation

Perhaps the hardest authority for Scripture to be placed over is the most
intimate one: your own feelings and inner sense of what feels true, right,
or loving. Modern culture often treats inner feeling as the final,
unquestionable authority — "follow your heart," "trust your truth." But
Jeremiah's assessment of the human heart is sobering: it is deceitful, and
even to the person feeling it, hard to fully understand or trust.

This doesn't mean feelings are worthless or that Christians should try to
suppress all emotion — Scripture itself is full of raw, honest emotional
expression, especially in the Psalms. It means feelings, like reason and
tradition, need to be tested against Scripture rather than trusted
automatically as accurate guides to truth or to God's will. A feeling of
certainty isn't the same as being right. A feeling of love isn't proof
that a choice is wise or good. Emotions can be genuine responses to real
things, or they can be, in Jeremiah's words, deceitful — and often it's
hard to tell which from the inside.

Submitting your feelings to Scripture's authority means asking, especially
in moments of strong conviction or desire, "does this actually align with
what God has said?" — rather than assuming the strength of the feeling
itself is evidence of its trustworthiness.

## Prayer

Lord, my heart is not always a reliable guide, however certain it feels.
Teach me to test my feelings against your word rather than trusting them
automatically. Anchor me in what you've said, not just what I sense. Amen.

## Reflection Question

Is there a strong feeling or conviction you're currently trusting that you
haven't actually tested against what Scripture says?
