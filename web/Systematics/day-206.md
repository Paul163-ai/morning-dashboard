## Verse

"There is therefore now no condemnation for those who are in Christ
Jesus." — Romans 8:1

## Explanation

We close this week on the note justification exists to produce: assurance.
If justification is a genuine legal verdict — righteous, declared once and
fully — then Paul's conclusion follows necessarily: no condemnation. Not
"less condemnation," not "condemnation held at bay as long as you perform
well," but none. The case is closed. There is no future court date where
this verdict could be reopened and overturned.

This should reshape how a Christian responds to their own ongoing sin and
failure. Conviction is right and healthy — the Spirit still grieves over
sin in a believer's life, and repentance remains necessary daily, as we
saw earlier this month. But condemnation — the crushing, hopeless sense
that you are once again on trial, that God's verdict over you is now in
question — has no legal ground to stand on if justification is real. Satan
may accuse; your own conscience may accuse; but the Judge has already
ruled, and no one can bring a successful charge against those God has
justified (Romans 8:33–34).

If guilt over old failures still returns and feels like fresh condemnation
rather than an invitation to repentance, that feeling is not describing
your actual legal status before God. Return to this verse. The verdict
stands: no condemnation, for those in Christ Jesus — today, and every day
after.

## Prayer

Father, thank you that there is no condemnation left for me in Christ.
When guilt returns and tries to convince me otherwise, remind me that the
verdict is already settled. Let conviction lead me to repentance, not to
despair. Amen.

## Reflection Question

When guilt resurfaces over old sin, does it usually function as healthy
conviction leading to repentance, or as condemnation that denies the
verdict already settled? How can you tell the difference next time?
