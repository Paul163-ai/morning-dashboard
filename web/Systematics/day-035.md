## Verse

"Hear, O Israel: The Lord our God, the Lord is one." — Deuteronomy 6:4

## Explanation

It's easy to hear "Father, Son, and Holy Spirit" and quietly slide toward
imagining three separate gods working together, like a divine committee.
Scripture rules this out firmly and repeatedly. Israel's most fundamental
confession, recited daily, was radical monotheism: the Lord is one — not
one among many, not a chief god among lesser gods, but the only God there
is. The New Testament never abandons this; it simply reveals that this
one God exists, eternally, as three persons.

The precise language the church settled on, after centuries of careful
biblical reflection, distinguishes "person" from "being" or "essence."
There is one divine being — one God, undivided — and within that one
being, three distinct persons, each fully and equally God, not three
separate gods and not three modes or masks worn by a single person at
different times (a heresy called modalism). The Father is not the Son;
the Son is not the Spirit; yet Father, Son, and Spirit are, together, the
one true God.

This distinction — one being, three persons — isn't philosophical
hair-splitting. It's the difference between the biblical God and both
polytheism (many gods) and a unitarian god without real internal
relationship. Getting this right matters for everything that follows.

## Prayer

Lord, you are one God, yet Father, Son, and Spirit — really distinct, yet
never divided. Guard my understanding from error in either direction, and
let this mystery deepen my worship rather than confuse it. Amen.

## Reflection Question

Have you ever unconsciously thought of the Trinity as three separate gods
working together, rather than one God existing as three persons?
