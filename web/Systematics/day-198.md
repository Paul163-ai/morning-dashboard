## Verse

"Godly grief produces a repentance that leads to salvation without regret,
whereas worldly grief produces death." — 2 Corinthians 7:10

## Explanation

Paul draws a sharp line between two kinds of sorrow over sin, and it's
worth learning to tell them apart. Worldly grief is sorrow over
consequences — getting caught, losing face, damaging a relationship, feeling
ashamed. It's real sorrow, but it's self-focused, and Paul says bluntly
that it leads nowhere good; it "produces death," not change. Godly grief is
different in kind: sorrow over having offended God himself, grief that
sin is sin regardless of who noticed or what it cost you.

You can often tell which kind of grief you're experiencing by what happens
next. Worldly grief tends to fade once the immediate consequence passes,
or it curdles into self-pity and defensiveness. Godly grief moves toward
actual change — toward God, in humility, and away from the sin itself —
because the grief was never really about being caught in the first place.

This matters because repentance is a gift, not a self-generated
performance (2 Timothy 2:25). If you find yourself only ever sorry when
consequences catch up with you, ask God for what you cannot manufacture
yourself: a heart that grieves sin because it grieves him, and that turns
not out of fear of exposure, but out of love for the one it has wronged.

## Prayer

Father, examine my sorrow over sin. Where it has only ever been about
consequences, give me instead a grief that grieves you — and repentance
that actually leads somewhere. I cannot manufacture this in myself; I ask
you for it as a gift. Amen.

## Reflection Question

Think of the last time you felt sorry for a sin. Was it grief over
offending God, or grief over consequences? Be honest with yourself.
