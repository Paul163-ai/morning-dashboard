## Verse

"Not having a righteousness of my own that comes from the law, but that
which comes through faith in Christ, the righteousness from God that
depends on faith." — Philippians 3:9

## Explanation

Paul writes this after listing, just verses earlier, an impressive resume
by any Jewish religious standard — pedigree, zeal, blameless law-keeping.
He calls all of it "rubbish" (v.8) compared to what he's after: not a
righteousness he could point to as his own achievement, but one that comes
entirely from outside himself, through faith in Christ.

This is where the doctrines of the last few weeks land in real, personal
terms. It's one thing to affirm imputed righteousness as a doctrine. It's
another thing to actually rest there, especially on days when your own
record — your effort, your obedience, your spiritual performance — feels
thin or disappointing. The temptation, even for mature believers, is to
quietly slide back toward "a righteousness of my own," measuring your
standing before God by how the day, the week, or the season has gone.

Paul's language is deliberately relational, not just legal: he wants to
"gain Christ and be found in him." Resting in imputed righteousness isn't
a cold transaction to accept intellectually once and then forget. It's a
posture to return to daily — actively refusing to build your confidence
before God on anything you produced, and consciously resting instead on
what Christ has already supplied.

## Prayer

Lord, like Paul, I want to count anything of my own as nothing compared to
knowing you and being found in your righteousness, not mine. On the days I
feel most disappointing to myself, remind me my standing rests on you.
Amen.

## Reflection Question

On your hardest, most spiritually disappointing days, where do you tend
to look for reassurance — your own effort, or Christ's righteousness?
