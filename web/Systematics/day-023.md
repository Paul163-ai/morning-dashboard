## Verse

"These are written so that you may believe that Jesus is the Christ, the
Son of God, and that by believing you may have life in his name." —
John 20:31

## Explanation

Notice why John says he wrote his Gospel: not to display literary
sophistication, or to be understood only by trained interpreters, but so
that ordinary readers would believe and have life. That's the purpose
running through the whole of Scripture, and it explains why clarity isn't
an incidental feature — it's a deliberate mercy from a God who wants his
message actually received by the people he's speaking to.

Think about what the alternative would mean: a God who genuinely wanted
relationship with ordinary people, but who deliberately made the way to
know him obscure, technical, and reserved for the specially trained. That
would contradict everything else we've seen about God's character —
his goodness, his desire that none should perish (2 Peter 3:9), his
delight in revealing himself to "little children" rather than only the
wise and learned (Matthew 11:25). Clarity flows directly from God's own
gracious intention to actually be known, not merely admired from a
respectful, confused distance.

This should shape your own approach to Scripture with gratitude rather
than anxiety. God didn't write a book meant to keep you guessing. He wrote
one meant to be understood, believed, and lived — by you, as you are,
right now.

## Prayer

Father, thank you for wanting to be known, not hidden — for writing your
word so ordinary people like me could actually understand it and believe.
Let me approach Scripture with confidence in your good intention toward
me. Amen.

## Reflection Question

Does it change how you feel opening the Bible to think of its clarity as
a deliberate mercy from a God who wants to be known by you specifically?
