## Verse

"God sent forth his Son... to redeem those who were under the law, so that
we might receive adoption as sons." — Galatians 4:4–5

## Explanation

We finish July with a doctrine that's easy to underrate because it sounds
almost like a footnote to justification. It isn't. Justification changes
your legal record — no longer guilty, but righteous. Adoption changes
something else entirely: your relationship, your family, your identity.
You're not simply an acquitted defendant walking free from the courtroom.
You're a welcomed child, brought permanently into God's own household.

In the ancient world Paul was writing to, adoption carried enormous legal
weight: an adopted son had exactly the same rights, inheritance, and
standing as a biological son — sometimes even more secure, since an
adoption couldn't be undone the way a birth relationship could be
disowned. Paul reaches for this picture deliberately. Your place in God's
family isn't provisional, probationary, or lesser than if you'd somehow
been born into it. It's full, permanent, and legally established.

This adds something justification alone doesn't quite capture: warmth.
You are not simply declared not-guilty by a Judge. You are welcomed home
by a Father. Over these last two days of July, we'll trace what that
means for how you relate to God day to day, and what it secures for your
future.

## Prayer

Father, thank you that I'm not just acquitted but adopted — brought fully
into your family, with all the rights and welcome of a true child. Help me
relate to you today not as a nervous defendant, but as a beloved son or
daughter. Amen.

## Reflection Question

Do you tend to relate to God more like an acquitted defendant relieved to
be free, or a welcomed child at home in the family? What might shift that?
