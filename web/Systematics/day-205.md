## Verse

"To the one who does not work but believes in him who justifies the
ungodly, his faith is counted as righteousness." — Romans 4:5

## Explanation

This verse is almost scandalous if you read it slowly. God "justifies the
ungodly" — not the reformed, not the improved, not the person who's
finally gotten their act together, but the ungodly, as they are, the
moment they believe. This is exactly why justification has to be kept
distinct from sanctification, the subject we'll turn to shortly. They're
both real, both essential, both the work of God's grace — but they answer
different questions.

Justification answers: what is my legal status before God? Answer:
righteous, fully, right now, through faith in Christ. Sanctification
answers: how holy is my actual character and conduct becoming? Answer:
progressively, over a lifetime, with real ups and downs, never complete
until glory. Confusing the two causes real damage either way. Mistake
sanctification for justification, and you'll base your standing with God
on your daily performance, swinging between pride and despair. Mistake
justification for a licence to ignore sanctification, and you'll presume
on grace while never actually pursuing holiness (Romans 6:1–2).

Held rightly, the order matters enormously: justification comes first,
complete and unearned, and sanctification flows out of it, not toward it.
You're not becoming holy in order to become right with God. You're already
declared right with God, and that settled status is what makes pursuing
holiness possible at all.

## Prayer

Father, thank you that you justified me while I was still ungodly, not
after I cleaned myself up. Help me hold justification and sanctification
rightly — resting fully in the first, pursuing wholeheartedly the second.
Amen.

## Reflection Question

Do you ever confuse your progress in holiness with your standing before
God? How does today's distinction help untangle that?
