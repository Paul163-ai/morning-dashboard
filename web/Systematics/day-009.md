## Verse

"Men spoke from God as they were carried along by the Holy Spirit." —
2 Peter 1:21

## Explanation

This week we look more closely at a claim we touched on briefly in
January's first days: Scripture is, quite literally, God's own words —
not merely a human record about God that happens to be reliable, but words
that carry his direct authorship. How can that be true while also being
true that Moses, David, Isaiah, Paul, and John each wrote in their own
voice, vocabulary, and circumstances?

Theologians describe this as dual authorship, or concursus: God worked
through the personalities, research, experiences, and even the emotional
states of human authors, without overriding their humanity, such that what
resulted was fully their own writing and fully God's own word at the same
time. This isn't dictation, where the human is a passive pen in God's
hand — Luke tells us he carefully investigated sources (Luke 1:1–4); Paul
argues with visible personal urgency and even occasional exasperation.
Yet Peter is clear: none of it originated in mere human will. The Spirit
carried it.

This means when you read Paul's very human, sometimes emotional letters,
you're not reading around God's word to find it — you're reading God's
word, given exactly through Paul's genuine humanity, not despite it.

## Prayer

Holy Spirit, thank you for the mystery and mercy of carrying human authors
along so their real words became your word. Help me trust Scripture fully,
in both its humanity and its divine origin. Amen.

## Reflection Question

Does knowing Scripture has real human authors, with real personalities and
circumstances, make it feel less trustworthy to you, or more relatable?
Why?
