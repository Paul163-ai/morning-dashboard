## Verse

"All have sinned and fall short of the glory of God, and are justified by
his grace as a gift, through the redemption that is in Christ Jesus." —
Romans 3:23–24

## Explanation

Justification is one of the most precise, and most misunderstood, words in
the New Testament. It's a legal term, borrowed from the courtroom: to
"justify" someone is to declare them righteous, not to make them righteous
through some inner transformation. That distinction matters enormously.
Sanctification (which we'll come to later) is about actually becoming more
holy over time. Justification is different — it's an instant, complete,
legal verdict: righteous, right now, fully, in God's own courtroom.

This is why Paul can say in the very same breath that all have sinned and
fall short, and yet are justified. The verdict doesn't wait for the sin
problem to be fixed first through gradual improvement. It's granted "as a
gift," "by his grace," through what Christ has already accomplished. The
moment you trust Christ, God the Judge declares you righteous — not
because the record has changed, but because Christ's redemption has dealt
with it entirely.

This is enormously freeing if you've ever felt your standing with God
rises and falls with your daily performance. Justification isn't a status
you maintain by good behaviour or lose through failure. It's a legal
verdict, given once, resting entirely on Christ's finished work — a truth
worth returning to on your worst days, not just your best ones.

## Prayer

Father, thank you that my standing before you isn't a verdict I have to
keep earning day by day. You have declared me righteous in Christ, once
and completely. Let that truth steady me today, whatever kind of day it
has been. Amen.

## Reflection Question

Do you tend to feel more or less "right with God" depending on how your
day has gone? How does justification as a legal, settled verdict speak to
that?
