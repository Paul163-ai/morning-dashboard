## Verse

"Before the mountains were brought forth, or ever you had formed the
earth and the world, from everlasting to everlasting you are God." —
Psalm 90:2

## Explanation

God's eternity means he exists without beginning, without end, and
without being bound by the passage of time the way his creatures are.
Moses' prayer draws the contrast sharply: mountains, earth, the whole
world — all of it had a beginning, brought forth, formed. God did not.
"From everlasting to everlasting" describes existence outside and prior
to time itself, not simply existence that has lasted a very long time.

This is genuinely hard to picture, because every human experience is
saturated with time — we remember a past, anticipate a future, and
experience only a fleeting present moment at a time. God's relationship
to time is different in kind, not just in degree. He doesn't wait for
the future to arrive, the way you do; he doesn't lose the past to memory
alone, the way you do. Theologians differ on exactly how to describe
this relationship, but Scripture is consistent that God stands above
and beyond the limitations time places on every creature.

This matters more than it might first seem. A God bound by time the way
we are couldn't be fully present to every moment of history at once,
couldn't know the future with certainty, and couldn't be as reliable a
foundation for promises spanning generations as Scripture describes him
to be. His eternity is what makes his promises trustworthy across all of
time, including the time still ahead of you.

## Prayer

Eternal God, from everlasting to everlasting, thank you that you are not
bound by the limits of time the way I am. Let that truth give me
confidence in your promises for whatever time still lies ahead of me.
Amen.

## Reflection Question

Does thinking of God as existing entirely outside of time change how you
trust his promises for your future?
