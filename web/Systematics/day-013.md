## Verse

"Man shall not live by bread alone, but by every word that comes from the
mouth of God." — Matthew 4:4

## Explanation

If Scripture really is God's own word, as we spent last week
establishing, one conclusion necessarily follows: it holds final
authority. Not one voice among several to be weighed equally against
tradition, reason, culture, or personal experience — but the standard by
which all of those are themselves evaluated. When Jesus faced Satan's
temptations in the wilderness, hungry and alone, he didn't reason his way
out philosophically. He answered every temptation with "it is written" —
Scripture, applied directly, as final and sufficient authority.

Authority means the power to settle a matter. Scripture's authority means
that when it speaks clearly on something — God's character, human sin,
salvation in Christ, how to live — that settles the question. Not
"here's an ancient perspective worth considering," but "this is what is
actually true, because this is what God has said." Submitting to that
authority is very different from merely admiring or being inspired by the
Bible. It means letting it have the final word, even over your own
instincts.

This week we'll trace what that authority means in practice — over
tradition, reason, and experience — and why submitting to it, rather than
merely consulting it, is what it actually means to take Scripture
seriously.

## Prayer

Father, thank you for giving me a final word I can trust completely. Where
I've treated Scripture as one voice among many rather than the settling
authority, correct me this week. Amen.

## Reflection Question

Think of an area of life where you tend to reason things out yourself
first, and check Scripture second, if at all. What would it look like to
reverse that order?
