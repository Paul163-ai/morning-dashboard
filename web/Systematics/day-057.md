## Verse

"As for you, you meant evil against me, but God meant it for good, to
bring it about that many people should be kept alive, as they are
today." — Genesis 50:20

## Explanation

The hardest test of God's goodness isn't in seasons of blessing, when
gratitude comes easily — it's in seasons of genuine suffering, betrayal,
and loss, when goodness feels hardest to believe. Joseph's words to the
brothers who sold him into slavery, after decades of real suffering that
was never undone or erased, are remarkable precisely because they don't
minimise the evil done to him. He names it plainly: "you meant evil." And
yet, in the very same breath, he affirms God's good purpose working
even through it.

This isn't a formula that makes every specific suffering immediately
explicable — Joseph didn't understand God's purposes while he sat forgotten
in an Egyptian prison for years; he only saw them in hindsight, decades
later. Goodness in suffering usually isn't visible in the moment; it's
something trusted in the dark and often only partially understood, if at
all, much later, if ever, this side of eternity.

This is why God's goodness has to rest on his revealed character, not on
your ability to currently trace his purposes. Joseph could affirm God's
good intent looking backward. You may not yet be able to. But the same
God who was quietly, invisibly working good through Joseph's suffering
is the same unchanging, good God at work, even now, in whatever you
cannot yet see the purpose of.

## Prayer

Lord, I don't always see the good you're working, especially in
suffering. Give me Joseph's kind of trust — not because I can explain my
pain, but because I know your unchanging, good character. Amen.

## Reflection Question

Is there a past hardship where you can now see, like Joseph, some good
God brought from it? Does that give you any confidence for a current
struggle where you can't yet see it?
