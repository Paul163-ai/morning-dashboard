## Verse

"The secret things belong to the Lord our God, but the things that are
revealed belong to us and to our children forever." — Deuteronomy 29:29

## Explanation

Anyone who reads the Bible carefully will eventually meet passages that
seem, on the surface, to be in tension with one another, or difficult to
square with what's known from history or science. Taking inerrancy
seriously doesn't mean pretending these difficulties don't exist, or
forcing implausible harmonisations out of anxiety. It means approaching
them with humility and patience, trusting that the difficulty more often
lies in our limited understanding, our incomplete historical knowledge, or
our misreading of genre and context, rather than in Scripture's own
truthfulness.

Moses' words to Israel offer a helpful posture here: some things genuinely
remain secret, beyond our present ability to fully resolve — and that's
acceptable, even expected, given the gap between finite readers and
infinite revelation. The right response to an unresolved difficulty isn't
panic or quiet abandonment of confidence in Scripture; it's patient
study, held alongside a settled trust grounded in everything else we've
already seen about who God is and how trustworthy his character makes his
word.

Across church history, most apparent difficulties that once seemed
unanswerable have, over time, found solid resolution through better
historical, linguistic, or archaeological understanding. That track
record is itself worth remembering the next time a new difficulty
surfaces.

## Prayer

Lord, when I meet passages I can't yet reconcile, give me patience rather
than panic. Let my confidence rest in your proven character, even while
some things remain unresolved for now. Amen.

## Reflection Question

Is there a specific passage or apparent contradiction that has troubled
you? What would it look like to hold it with patience rather than
letting it erode your trust in Scripture as a whole?
