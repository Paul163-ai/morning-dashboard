## Verse

"For our sake he made him to be sin who knew no sin, so that in him we
might become the righteousness of God." — 2 Corinthians 5:21

## Explanation

We spent last week establishing that justification is real and complete.
This week, the question underneath it: on what basis can God justly
declare a sinner righteous? If God simply overlooked sin to declare people
righteous, he wouldn't be just — he'd be pretending. Something has to
actually account for the sin, and something has to actually supply the
righteousness. Paul describes both halves of the exchange in a single,
breathtaking verse.

Christ, sinless, was made to be sin — not that he became personally
guilty, but that our sin was legally credited to him, so that he bore its
full penalty on the cross. And in exchange, we — sinful, without merit of
our own — become the righteousness of God in him: his perfect righteousness
credited to our account, as though it were our own achievement. This is
sometimes called "the great exchange": our sin to Christ, his righteousness
to us.

This is the answer to yesterday's question. God can justly justify the
ungodly because the ungodly, in Christ, are no longer standing on their own
record at all. They're standing on Christ's. The verdict "righteous" isn't
a legal fiction or a polite overlooking — it's grounded in an actual
transfer, accomplished at the cross, as real as the sin it dealt with.

## Prayer

Lord Jesus, thank you for taking my sin so completely that your own
righteousness could become mine. I bring nothing to this exchange but my
need — you supplied everything else. Amen.

## Reflection Question

Does the idea of an actual "exchange" — your sin for his righteousness —
feel real and personal to you, or more like a theological formula? What
would help make it feel real?
