## Verse

"Can you find out the deep things of God? Can you find out the limit of
the Almighty? It is higher than heaven — what can you do? Deeper than
Sheol — what can you know?" — Job 11:7–8

## Explanation

January closed with the question of whether God exists. February opens
with a harder one: even granting that he exists, can we actually know
him? Zophar's questions to Job aren't rhetorical tricks — they're a sober
reminder that God, by definition, exceeds any finite mind's capacity to
fully grasp. This is what theologians call God's incomprehensibility: not
that God is unknowable, but that he can never be known exhaustively,
completely, the way you might fully understand a math equation or a fellow
human being with enough time and attention.

This should produce genuine humility in a whole year of studying
theology. Even after fifty-nine weeks together working through doctrine
after doctrine, we will not have come close to comprehending God as he
comprehends himself. Every true thing we learn about him this year is
real and reliable — but partial, like starlight reaching earth from a sun
we can never look at directly. Anyone who claims to have God entirely
figured out has, in that very claim, misunderstood who God is.

Tomorrow we'll look at the other half of this truth — that despite this
incomprehensibility, God can still be genuinely, truly known. Today, let
the size of the mystery itself lead you to worship rather than
frustration.

## Prayer

Lord, you are higher than heaven and deeper than I can fathom. Keep me
humble in a whole year of studying you, never assuming I've captured you
in my understanding. Let the mystery lead me to worship, not frustration.
Amen.

## Reflection Question

Where might overconfidence about "having God figured out" be quietly
