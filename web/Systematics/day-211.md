## Verse

"For you did not receive the spirit of slavery to fall back into fear, but
you have received the Spirit of adoption as sons, by whom we cry, 'Abba!
Father!'" — Romans 8:15–16

## Explanation

Paul contrasts two entirely different postures a person could have toward
God: slavery, marked by fear, and adoption, marked by intimate trust. A
slave serves out of dread of punishment, always uncertain of their
standing, always one failure away from disaster. A child — an adopted
child, fully secure in the family — serves out of love, not terror,
because their place in the household was never actually in question.

"Abba" is an intimate, familial word — close to how a small child would
address a father, trusting and unguarded rather than formal and distant.
Paul says this is the Spirit's own testimony inside a believer: not
occasionally, but as an ongoing cry, prompted by the Spirit himself,
bearing witness together with your own spirit that you genuinely belong to
God as his child (v.16).

If your prayer life often feels more like a nervous employee reporting to
a demanding boss than a child running to a loving father, that's worth
noticing. Adoption isn't just a legal fact to believe intellectually; it's
meant to reshape the emotional texture of how you approach God — with the
same unselfconscious confidence a small child has running toward a parent
they trust completely.

## Prayer

Father, thank you for the Spirit who lets me call you "Abba" — not with
formal distance, but with a child's trust. Where fear has crept into how I
approach you, replace it with the confidence of a beloved child. Amen.

## Reflection Question

When you pray, do you approach God more like a nervous employee or a
trusting child? What's one prayer you could pray today with a child's
confidence instead of fear?
