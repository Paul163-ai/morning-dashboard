## Verse

"For as by the one man's disobedience the many were made sinners, so by
the one man's obedience the many will be made righteous." — Romans 5:19

## Explanation

Paul draws a direct parallel between Adam and Christ that's worth slowing
down for. Through Adam's one act of disobedience in the garden, sin and
guilt were credited to the whole human race he represented — not because
everyone personally committed that exact sin, but because Adam acted as
humanity's covenant head, and his record became theirs (we touched on this
back when we studied original sin). Paul now says the same logic runs the
other way through Christ: through his one act of perfect obedience — a
whole life of obedience culminating at the cross — righteousness is
credited to everyone united to him by faith.

This is what theologians call the "active obedience" of Christ, alongside
his "passive obedience" (his suffering and death, which paid sin's
penalty). It's not enough that Christ merely absorbed punishment; he also
had to actually live the perfectly obedient life that we failed to live,
and that obedience becomes ours by imputation, credited to our account
just as surely as our sin was credited to his.

This means your righteousness before God was never meant to be
self-generated, even in principle. It was always going to have to come from
somewhere outside you — and it has. Christ didn't just cancel a debt. He
supplied, from his own obedient life, exactly what your record was
missing.

## Prayer

Father, thank you that Christ didn't only bear my punishment but also
lived the obedient life I failed to live, and gave me credit for it.
Every part of my righteousness before you comes from him, not from me.
Amen.

## Reflection Question

Have you thought of Christ's righteousness mainly in terms of his death
paying your penalty? How does adding his obedient life fill out that
picture?
