## Verse

"Jesus answered him, 'Truly, truly, I say to you, unless one is born again
he cannot see the kingdom of God.'" — John 3:3

## Explanation

Nicodemus was a serious, respected religious teacher, and Jesus told him
plainly that his knowledge, morality, and heritage counted for nothing
without something he could not produce himself: a new birth. Regeneration
is the theological word for this — the inward, spiritual re-creation God
performs in someone who was, until that moment, spiritually dead.

This is different from everything we've looked at this week so far.
Election is God's eternal choice; the gospel call is the outward invitation
you hear. Regeneration is the moment the invitation actually takes root —
God, by his Spirit, gives you a new nature, capable for the first time of
truly seeing, understanding, and desiring him. Nicodemus's confusion (verse
4 — "can a man enter his mother's womb a second time?") is exactly the
right reaction to something this radical. This isn't self-improvement. It's
new creation.

That's worth sitting with if you've ever felt discouraged trying to
"become" a better Christian through sheer effort before you understood
grace. The Christian life doesn't begin with you working harder to please
God. It begins with God doing something to you that you were entirely
unable to do for yourself — giving you a heart that can actually respond to
him.

## Prayer

Lord, thank you that my new life didn't begin with my own effort but with
your work in me. I could not have made myself alive to you — you did that.
Keep reminding me that grace, not striving, is where this all started.
Amen.

## Reflection Question

Do you ever slip into thinking your relationship with God depends on your
own effort to "become" spiritual? How does regeneration correct that?
