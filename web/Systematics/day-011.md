## Verse

"If anyone thinks that he is a prophet, or spiritual, he should acknowledge
that the things I am writing to you are a command of the Lord." —
1 Corinthians 14:37

## Explanation

The claim to divine authority doesn't stop with the Old Testament
prophets. Paul makes an extraordinary statement about his own letters: not
merely good advice or personal opinion, but "a command of the Lord."
Peter goes even further, placing Paul's letters in the same category as
"the other Scriptures" (2 Peter 3:15–16) — a remarkable, almost casual
acknowledgment that the New Testament apostolic writings were already
being received as Scripture alongside the Old Testament, within the
lifetime of the apostles themselves.

This matters because it completes the picture. The Bible you hold isn't
half-Scripture (the Old Testament, clearly divine) and half-reliable
history (the New Testament, merely a trustworthy record). Both halves
claim, and were received by the early church as carrying, the same divine
authority — the prophets speaking for God under the old covenant, and the
apostles speaking for Christ under the new, each carried along by the same
Spirit.

This is why a Christian can read Paul's letters, or John's Gospel, with
the same confidence they'd bring to reading Isaiah or the Psalms: not
merely admirable early Christian writing, but God's own authoritative
word, given through chosen apostolic authors.

## Prayer

Lord, thank you for giving your church not just prophets but apostles,
carrying your authority into the new covenant. Let me receive the whole
New Testament with the same confidence I give the Old. Amen.

## Reflection Question

Is there a New Testament letter or passage you've treated more like
"good advice" than like Scripture carrying full divine authority?
