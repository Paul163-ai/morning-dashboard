## Verse

"He makes his sun rise on the evil and on the good, and sends rain on the
just and on the unjust." — Matthew 5:45

## Explanation

Before we talk about how God saves specific people, we need to talk about
something wider: God is kind to everyone, saved or not. Theologians call this
"common grace" — grace that is common to all humanity, as opposed to the
"saving grace" given only to believers. It doesn't forgive sin or reconcile
anyone to God. But it is still real grace: undeserved kindness from a holy
God to a rebellious world.

Common grace is why the world is not as bad as it could be. It's why
unbelievers can be genuinely loving parents, brilliant scientists, honest
neighbours, or gifted artists. It's why crops grow and rain falls on believer
and unbeliever's fields alike. None of it saves. But all of it is God
restraining sin's full effects and scattering good gifts across a world that
has turned from him.

This matters for how you see the people around you who don't yet know
Christ. Their kindness, their skill, their insight isn't something to be
suspicious of or explained away — it's evidence of God's ongoing, patient
generosity toward them, and often the very kindness meant to lead them
toward him (Romans 2:4). Recognising common grace should make Christians
more grateful, not less generous, toward a world God has not yet abandoned.

## Prayer

Father, thank you for your kindness to a world that does not honour you —
including me before I knew you. Help me see your common grace at work in the
people around me, and let it move me to gratitude rather than suspicion.
Amen.

## Reflection Question

Where have you seen genuine goodness in someone who doesn't yet believe? How
does calling that "common grace" change how you think about them?
