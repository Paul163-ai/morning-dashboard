## Verse

"Do you presume on the riches of his kindness and forbearance and patience,
not knowing that God's kindness is meant to lead you to repentance?" —
Romans 2:4

## Explanation

We've spent two days on God's kindness to all people. Today's the necessary
correction: common grace, however real, never saves anyone. It restrains
sin; it doesn't remove it. It gives good gifts; it doesn't give a new heart.
A person can enjoy every blessing of common grace — health, family,
success, even a love of beauty and truth — and still be estranged from God,
still under judgment, still in need of the gospel.

Paul's warning in Romans 2 is exactly this danger: mistaking God's patience
for approval. It's possible to receive kindness after kindness from God and
read it as evidence that everything is fine between you and him, when
really every undeserved gift is meant to be doing something else entirely —
leading you toward repentance, not away from your need for it.

So common grace and saving grace are not two ends of a spectrum, as if
enough of one becomes the other. They're different in kind. Saving grace
requires blood, a cross, faith, and a new birth — subjects we turn to
starting tomorrow. Common grace is the patient kindness that keeps a person
alive and functioning long enough to hear that gospel and respond to it.
Don't let the gift be mistaken for the rescue.

## Prayer

Father, forgive me for the times I've mistaken your patience for approval.
Thank you for every undeserved kindness in my life — and thank you even more
for the saving grace that actually rescued me. Keep me from resting in
lesser gifts when you offer yourself. Amen.

## Reflection Question

Is there an area of blessing in your life you've quietly treated as proof
that "things are fine" with God, rather than as a call to draw nearer to him?
