## Verse

"But Peter said, 'Ananias, why has Satan filled your heart to lie to the
Holy Spirit... You have not lied to man but to God.'" — Acts 5:3–4

## Explanation

The Spirit is often the least considered person of the Trinity in
ordinary Christian thinking, sometimes reduced in people's minds to a
vague force or influence rather than a full, personal member of the
Godhead. Scripture doesn't allow that. Peter's rebuke to Ananias makes
the equation explicit and immediate: lying to the Holy Spirit is lying to
God — not a lesser offence against a lesser being, but sin against God
himself.

The Spirit is also clearly personal, not merely an impersonal force —
Scripture describes him grieving (Ephesians 4:30), interceding
(Romans 8:26), teaching (John 14:26), and speaking (Acts 13:2), all
things an impersonal power cannot do. And he shares fully in the works
and attributes unique to God: present everywhere (Psalm 139:7),
all-knowing (1 Corinthians 2:10–11), active in creation (Genesis 1:2) and
in the resurrection of Christ (Romans 8:11).

This completes the picture we've built across the week: Father, Son, and
Spirit, three distinct persons, each fully, equally God, together the one
true God Israel confessed and the New Testament reveals. As you close
this week, let it move you toward the same posture the earliest Christians
had: worship of the whole Trinity, not merely acknowledgment of the
Father, admiration of the Son, and vague awareness of the Spirit.

## Prayer

Holy Spirit, forgive me for the times I've treated you as an impersonal
force rather than fully God, worthy of the same worship as the Father and
the Son. Teach me to know and honour you as you truly are. Amen.

## Reflection Question

Of the three persons of the Trinity, which has felt least real or
personal to you in your own faith? What might it look like to grow there?
