## Verse

"To all who did receive him, who believed in his name, he gave the right to
become children of God, who were born, not of blood nor of the will of the
flesh nor of the will of man, but of God." — John 1:12–13

## Explanation

John is careful here to rule out every ordinary explanation for how someone
becomes a child of God. Not blood — it's not inherited through family
lineage or upbringing in a Christian home. Not the will of the flesh — it's
not produced by human willpower or self-effort. Not the will of man — no
other person, however influential, can manufacture it in you. It is, John
says simply, "of God." Regeneration is his act alone, hidden and
sovereign, like wind that "blows where it wishes" (John 3:8) — you see its
effects without controlling its source.

This is why regeneration is usually described as instantaneous and secret.
You don't watch it happen the way you'd watch a plant grow. There isn't a
moment you consciously experience God "doing the surgery." What you
experience is the result: sudden or gradual awareness that you now believe
what once seemed foolish, want what you once resisted, and receive Christ
where you once turned away. The change is visible; its cause, hidden in God.

This should relieve any pressure to locate a precise, dramatic memory of
"the moment I was regenerated" as proof of your salvation. What matters is
not that you can pinpoint the invisible act of God, but that its fruit —
faith, and a welcome for Christ — is present in you now.

## Prayer

Father, thank you that my new birth rests entirely on your will, not mine.
I don't need to trace every detail of when or how it happened — I only
need to see its fruit: that I believe and receive Christ today. Amen.

## Reflection Question

Do you feel pressure to identify an exact, dramatic "moment" of conversion?
How does this passage free you from needing that certainty?
