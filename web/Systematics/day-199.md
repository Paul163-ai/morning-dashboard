## Verse

"Remember therefore from where you have fallen; repent, and do the works
you did at first." — Revelation 2:5

## Explanation

This wasn't written to unbelievers — it was written to a church, people
who had already believed, already turned to Christ once. Yet Jesus still
calls them to repent. That should settle the question of whether repentance
is a one-time event or an ongoing pattern in the Christian life. It's both:
a decisive turning at conversion, and a repeated posture for as long as you
live, because sin never stops needing to be turned from.

The Ephesian church hadn't fallen into some dramatic scandal — their
specific failure was abandoning their first love (v.4), a quiet drift
rather than a sudden collapse. That's usually how repentance is needed most
in the Christian life: not in response to one obvious crisis, but in
steady course-correction against slow drift — coldness creeping into
prayer, love for Christ fading into routine, old sins quietly resurfacing.

Martin Luther's first of his Ninety-Five Theses put it memorably: when
Christ said "repent," he willed the entire life of believers to be one of
repentance. That's not a grim call to constant guilt. It's an invitation to
keep coming back, again and again, to the one who blots out sin and
restores what drifts. A Christian life without ongoing repentance isn't
maturity — it's usually just a church, or a heart, that has stopped
noticing its own drift.

## Prayer

Lord, show me where I've quietly drifted — from you, from first love, from
obedience I once pursued eagerly. Give me the humility to keep turning back
to you, today and every day. Amen.

## Reflection Question

Where might you have drifted quietly, without a single dramatic moment
you could point to? What would turning back look like this week?
